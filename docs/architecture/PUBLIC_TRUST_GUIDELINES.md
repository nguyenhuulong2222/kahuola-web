# Kahu Ola Public Trust Guidelines

Kahu Ola is a civic hazard intelligence platform serving the residents of Hawaiʻi.

Public trust is the most critical factor for the effectiveness of the system.

These guidelines define how hazard information must be communicated to ensure clarity, responsibility, and cultural respect.

---

# Principle 1 — Clarity Over Complexity

Hazard communication must be understandable within seconds.

The dashboard must prioritize:

• simple language  
• clear hazard states  
• visual clarity  

Technical details should be secondary to user comprehension.

---

# Principle 2 — The 5-Second Rule

Users must understand the hazard situation within five seconds of viewing the dashboard.

Essential questions the interface must answer immediately:

Is there danger near me?  
What type of hazard is occurring?  
How serious is the situation?

---

# Principle 3 — Avoid Panic Language

The system must avoid language that creates unnecessary panic.

Examples of discouraged language:

"Catastrophic event imminent"  
"Extreme disaster approaching"

Preferred language:

"Elevated wildfire activity detected"  
"Heavy rainfall may cause flooding"

Hazard communication must remain calm and factual.

---

# Principle 4 — Clearly Distinguish Authority

Kahu Ola provides situational awareness.

It does not issue official emergency instructions.

All pages must clearly link to official authorities:

HIEMA  
County Emergency Management  
National Weather Service  

Users must never confuse the dashboard with an official command system.

---

# Principle 5 — Explain Uncertainty

Hazard data may contain uncertainty.

Examples:

Satellite fire detections may represent:

• agricultural burns  
• industrial heat sources  
• small fires  

The interface must explain that satellite detections are not confirmed fires.

Transparency about uncertainty builds trust.

---

# Principle 6 — Respect Cultural Context

Kahu Ola serves Hawaiʻi communities.

The platform should reflect Hawaiian cultural values.

Guiding principle:

E mālama pono.

Care for people, land, and community.

---

# Principle 7 — Honest Degraded States

If the system experiences limited data availability, the interface must say so.

Users must never be misled into believing the system is fully operational when it is not.

Degraded states must clearly indicate:

• which data sources are unavailable  
• whether signals may be outdated  

Honesty is more important than appearance of reliability.

---

# Principle 8 — Avoid Information Overload

Too much data can reduce comprehension.

The interface must prioritize the most important signals.

Dashboard structure:

Hero hazard signal  
Secondary signals  
Context indicators  
Map overview  

Additional technical details should be optional.

---

# Principle 9 — Encourage Official Preparedness

The platform should promote preparedness resources.

Examples:

ReadyHawaii.gov  
County evacuation information  
Emergency planning guides  

Providing preparation information strengthens community resilience.

---

# Principle 10 — Maintain Civic Neutrality

Kahu Ola must remain politically neutral.

The platform exists solely to provide hazard awareness and public safety information.

It must never be used for political messaging.

---

# Trust Commitment

Kahu Ola commits to:

• transparency  
• accuracy  
• humility  
• cultural respect  

Public trust must always be treated as a critical infrastructure asset.

---

# Cultural Commitment

E mālama pono.

Care for people, land, and community through responsible information systems.
